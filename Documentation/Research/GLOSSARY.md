## Astrophysics & Orbital Mechanics

**Delta-v**: The measure of impulse per unit of spacecraft mass required to perform a maneuver, measured in meters per second (m/s). Delta-v is the standard "currency" for evaluating the cost of orbital maneuvers.

**Hohmann Transfer**: A fuel-efficient orbital transfer consisting of two engine burns - one to depart the initial orbit and one to enter the destination orbit. Most efficient for transfers between coplanar, circular orbits.

**Inclination Change**: The process of changing the angle of an orbit relative to a reference plane, typically requiring significant delta-v expenditure.

**Lagrange Points**: The five positions (L1-L5) in an orbital system where gravitational forces balance in a way that allows objects to maintain relatively stable positions. L4 and L5 are the most stable.

**Orbital Perturbations**: Deviations from a Keplerian orbit caused by factors such as non-spherical gravity fields, atmospheric drag, solar radiation pressure, and third-body gravitational effects.

**Orbital Elements**: The six parameters (semi-major axis, eccentricity, inclination, longitude of ascending node, argument of periapsis, and true anomaly) that uniquely define an orbit.

**Patched Conic Approximation**: A method for approximating interplanetary trajectories by treating the spacecraft as being influenced by only one gravitational body at a time.

**Prograde Orbit**: An orbit in the same direction as the planet's rotation.

**Retrograde Orbit**: An orbit opposite to the direction of the planet's rotation.

**Transfer Window**: The optimal time period to initiate an interplanetary transfer to minimize delta-v requirements, determined by the relative positions of departure and destination bodies.

## Human Factors & Medicine

**Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS)**: A serious illness caused by exposure to high doses of radiation over a short period, resulting in symptoms including nausea, vomiting, headache, and potentially death.

**Artificial Gravity**: The simulation of gravitational forces in space environments, typically created through centripetal force in rotating habitats.

**Bone Demineralization**: The loss of bone mineral density experienced in microgravity at a rate of 1-2% per month without countermeasures, primarily affecting weight-bearing bones.

**Cardiovascular Deconditioning**: The degradation of cardiovascular function due to reduced load in microgravity, resulting in reduced blood volume, cardiac atrophy, and orthostatic intolerance upon return to gravity.

**Fluid Shift**: The redistribution of bodily fluids toward the upper body in microgravity due to the absence of hydrostatic pressure, causing facial edema, nasal congestion, and increased intracranial pressure.

**Muscle Atrophy**: The reduction in muscle mass and strength in microgravity, occurring at a rate of 5-12% per month for leg muscles without countermeasures.

**Orthostatic Intolerance**: The inability to maintain blood pressure in an upright position after exposure to microgravity, resulting in lightheadedness, dizziness, and potential fainting upon return to gravity.

**Partial Gravity**: Gravitational conditions between microgravity and Earth's gravity (e.g., Mars at 0.38g or Luna at 0.16g).

**Space Adaptation Syndrome (SAS)**: A form of motion sickness affecting approximately 70% of astronauts during the first few days in microgravity, characterized by nausea, disorientation, and vomiting.

**Spaceflight Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome (SANS)**: A condition affecting 15-30% of astronauts involving optic disc edema, globe flattening, and vision changes related to intracranial pressure changes in microgravity.

## Technology & Engineering

**Bioregenerative Life Support System**: A life support system that uses plants or algae to recycle air, water, and waste products through biological processes.

**Closed-Loop Life Support System**: A life support system that recycles air, water, and waste with minimal external inputs.

**Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS)**: The integrated system that maintains a safe and habitable environment in spacecraft, including air revitalization, water recovery, waste management, temperature and humidity control.

**In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)**: The practice of using resources found at the destination (e.g., Martian regolith, lunar ice) for life support, propellant, or construction.

**Mass Fraction**: The ratio of propellant mass to total spacecraft mass, a key metric for spacecraft efficiency.

**Nuclear Thermal Propulsion (NTP)**: A propulsion system that uses a nuclear reactor to heat propellant (typically hydrogen), achieving higher specific impulse than chemical rockets.

**Specific Impulse (Isp)**: A measure of propulsion efficiency represented as thrust divided by weight flow rate, expressed in seconds. Higher values indicate more efficient propulsion.

**Self-Replicating System**: A system capable of producing copies of itself, partially or completely, from available resources. In space applications, these systems range from simple component fabrication to full-scale autonomous manufacturing, with applications in habitat expansion, remote infrastructure development, and long-duration missions.

**Technology Readiness Level (TRL)**: A scale from 1-9 used to assess the maturity of a technology, where 1 represents basic principles observed and 9 represents flight-proven technology.

**Tensile Habitat**: A habitat design using tension-bearing materials (like fabrics) for structure, often inflatable or expandable.

**Vacuum Deposition**: A manufacturing process conducted in vacuum conditions, often used for applying thin coatings or films to materials.

**MADV (Mars Ascent/Descent Vehicle)**: A reusable, single-stage lander designed by Lockheed Martin for transporting crew from orbit to the Martian surface and back, using supersonic retropropulsion for descent and ascent.

**HAVOC (High Altitude Venus Operational Concept)**: A NASA concept for crewed habitats deployed at 50-60 km altitude in Venus' atmosphere, where temperature and pressure conditions are relatively Earth-like despite the extreme surface environment.

**MMX (Martian Moons eXploration)**: A JAXA mission to explore and return samples from Phobos, one of Mars' moons, providing technological foundations for human missions to these low-gravity bodies.

## Sociology, Politics & Economics

**Autonomous Colony**: A settlement that maintains self-governance independent from Earth-based authorities.

**Claim Jumping**: The illegal seizure or theft of space resources or territory claimed by another entity.

**Common Heritage Principle**: The concept that certain areas or resources belong to all humanity and cannot be claimed by individual nations, derived from the Outer Space Treaty and Moon Treaty.

**Faction**: A distinct social, political, or economic group with shared interests, values, or objectives within a space settlement.

**Generational Divergence**: The progressive separation in values, language, and cultural identity between Earth-born settlers and subsequent generations born in space.

**Non-Monetary Exchange System**: Economic systems not based on currency, such as reputation economies, energy credit systems, or resource allocation systems often used in closed space habitats.

**Resource Nationalism**: The assertion of sovereign rights over space resources by national governments, often in conflict with common heritage principles.

**Social Currency**: Non-monetary systems of value based on contribution, reputation, or social capital, often emerging in small space communities.

**Space-Born Culture**: Distinctive cultural elements that emerge among populations born and raised in space environments, including unique values, norms, and traditions.

**Technocracy**: A governance system where decision-makers are selected based on technical expertise rather than popular vote or appointment.

## AI & Information Systems

**Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**: An AI system with human-equivalent reasoning capabilities across multiple domains.

**Authority Gradient**: The power differential between individuals that affects willingness to question decisions or report concerns, particularly relevant in human-AI interactions.

**Autonomous System**: A system capable of executing functions and adapting to circumstances with minimal human intervention.

**Cognitive Load**: The total mental effort being used in working memory, a key consideration in UI design and human-AI interaction.

**Crew-Centered Automation**: Automation designed to augment human capabilities while keeping humans in decision loops, as opposed to replacing human functions.

**Digital Twin**: A virtual representation of a physical object or system that can be used for simulation, testing, and predictive maintenance.

**Human-AI Teaming**: Collaborative work between humans and AI systems where each brings complementary capabilities to achieve objectives.

**Mental Model**: The cognitive framework that allows humans to understand, explain, and predict system behavior and interactions.

**Modal Interface**: An interface where the same control produces different effects depending on the current operational mode.

**Overtrust**: The inappropriate reliance on automation beyond its actual capabilities, potentially leading to mission-critical errors.

**Situation Awareness**: The perception of environmental elements, comprehension of their meaning, and projection of their future states.

**Trust Calibration**: The process of aligning trust levels with the actual capabilities and reliability of an automated system.

## Combat & Strategy

**Ablative Armor**: Protective material designed to gradually vaporize under heat or impact, dissipating energy away from the protected structure.

**Directed Energy Weapon (DEW)**: A weapon that damages targets with focused energy rather than kinetic impact, including lasers, particle beams, and microwave weapons.

**Electronic Warfare (EW)**: Military actions involving the use of electromagnetic and directed energy to control the electromagnetic spectrum or attack the enemy.

**Fractionated System**: A system distributed across multiple physically separated units that function together, improving redundancy and survivability.

**Heat Signature**: The infrared radiation emitted by a spacecraft, particularly from propulsion and power systems, which can be detected by sensors.

**Hohmann Window**: The optimal timing for initiating an intercepting Hohmann transfer to reach a target, a key concept in orbital combat.

**Kinetic Energy Weapon (KEW)**: A weapon that damages targets through kinetic impact rather than explosives, such as tungsten rods, railguns, or coilguns.

**Kill Chain**: The sequence of events needed to complete an attack: target identification, tracking, weapon assignment, engagement, and damage assessment.

**Orbital Advantage**: A favorable position in orbital space providing tactical benefits such as energy efficiency, sensor coverage, or attack opportunities.

**Point Defense System**: Close-range defensive systems designed to destroy incoming projectiles or missiles before they reach their target.

**Sensor Fusion**: The combination of data from multiple sensors to achieve more accurate information than would be possible using individual sensors.

**Stealth in Space**: Techniques to reduce detectability in space environment, including thermal management, radar cross-section reduction, and emission control.

## Simulation & Game Mechanics

**Emergent Gameplay**: Complex gameplay arising from the interaction of relatively simple game mechanics.

**Fidelity**: The degree to which a simulation reproduces the exact behavior of the real-world system it models.

**n-Body Problem**: The problem of predicting the motion of celestial bodies interacting through gravitational forces, a fundamental challenge in accurate orbital mechanics simulation.

**Newtonian Simulation**: Physics simulation based on classical Newtonian mechanics, as opposed to relativistic or quantum effects.

**Patched Conics Method**: A simplified orbital mechanics model that treats a spacecraft as being influenced by only one gravitational body at a time.

**Procedural Generation**: The algorithmic creation of game content rather than manual design, often used for planetary surfaces, resource distribution, etc.

**Simulation Accuracy-Performance Tradeoff**: The balance between physical accuracy and computational performance in simulation design.

**Time Acceleration**: The ability to run simulation time faster than real-time, essential for space games involving long transit times.

**Time Dilation**: The difference in elapsed time measured by observers in different gravitational potentials or relative velocities, according to the theory of relativity.

**Two-Line Element Set (TLE)**: A data format encoding a list of orbital elements for an Earth-orbiting object at a given point in time.
